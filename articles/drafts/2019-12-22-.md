This blog post is largely based on a seminar paper I wrote together with Dominik Poß for the topics course on Bayesian Statistics with [Paul Heidhues](http://www.dice.hhu.de/diceteam/professorinnen-und-professoren/prof-dr-paul-heidhues.html) at Bonn University.

## 1. Observing Functional Data

In a previous post we discussed functional principal component analysis. Although the mathematical theory is well formulated, we can not directly apply this theory to given data set of curves: usually, one can only measure the outcome of the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th sample curve ![X_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-998dba373bb08119e12a646c873be88c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") on a discretized sequence of ![T_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-775d5b0840fab1ca58e1ac12c9ee64d2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") timepoints ![t_{i1},\dots,t_{iT_i} \in I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-bd8fc9bb320ef446464ab21a10ed114d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and hence, we are not able to observe the whole functions. In practice, these timepoints can be equidistant or they can be random and even worse: they can be different for each sample. Another serious problem we will have to encounter in many applications, is the fact that we only have noisy observation of the actual curves we are interested in.\\\
In our context, the challenge for a statistician is to find a way to make the above mentioned theory work such that we can construct a functional principal component analysis (FPCA) in practice. It turns out, that the problem of discrete data can be simply solved by replacing the integrals with sums. Theoretical results concerning the accuracy of this “`discretization”‘ process can for example be found in \cite{Hall2006}.\\\
So, in practice we actually observe data points ![Y_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-611bed68a54fa0a74f5125bc09cb8238_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") where\
\bes\
Y\_{ij}: = Y\_i(t\_{ij}) = X\_i(t\_{ij}) + \epsilon\_{ij} \quad, i=(1,…,N), j=(1,…,T\_i)\
\ees\
with ![\epsilon_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7cba15897659074c320d441ac9aaedb7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") being an error term which is often interpreted as a measurement error and is assumed to be iid. with zero mean and variance ![\sigma^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6a987274197f5fb6bfd3855d351bc2af_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\\
During a first step, the covariance ![C](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f34f74d98915e33f37a086f8cbfb996a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is replaced by the sample covariance matrix ![C_{sample}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b42578627d8d5a8fd2c85e327dfb30e9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), the empirical covariance operator. If each curve is observed over the same sequence of time points (i.e. equidistant design), this matrix is well defined by\
\bes\
C\_{sample}(t\_{j},t\_{k})= N^{-1} \sum\_{i=1}^{N} X\_i(t\_{j}) X\_i(t\_{k}).\
\ees\
If the design is random, one first has to focus on some smoothing or interpolation of the data using for example splines or local polynomial regression to get a proper empirical covariance operator – see for example \cite{Benko2009}, \cite{Besse1986} for a more detailed discussion.\\\
The resulting eigenvectors ![\hat{\gamma}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-621b1644d9381469bc760459852c2979_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") of the empirical covariance operator ![C_{sample}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b42578627d8d5a8fd2c85e327dfb30e9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") will be used as an estimate for the basisfunctions ![\gamma_k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-566615c9af5caaf0f8bfe4fe03cf1676_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and the corresponding loadings are estimated via the formula ![\hat{\theta}_{ik}=\sum_j \phi_k(t_{ij})X_i(t_{ij})dt](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-25df88e6a1ff2546c03bd4aa9f415535_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\\
Additionally, the assumption that the curves are already demeaned is not very reasonable when we face real data. This implies that we have to make use of some suitable estimator for the mean function ![E(X(t))=\mu(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-789cf96089ee943ebab17189ebb8806f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to center the data before performing the FPCA. A natural choice appears to use the sample mean as an estimator of the mean function. As shown in \cite{rice1991estimating}, this procedure converges for ![T=min(T_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ed0eb29063aa86db62567eaf95c856d8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") ![T \rightarrow \infty](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b57896efc7f80db13d8f1b5da96baea7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\ Even if we face data with additional white noise with variance ![\sigma^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6a987274197f5fb6bfd3855d351bc2af_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), a suitable smoothing as presented in \cite{rice1991estimating} will result in convergence for ![T \rightarrow \infty](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b57896efc7f80db13d8f1b5da96baea7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\\
But the problems of applying a FPCA with real data can get even worse: the above mentioned approach fails when the timepoints are sparse. Encountering sparse timepoints means that we only a have few observations for each curve which number, additionally, will be not even the same for all curves. One of the problems in this case is that we cannot plausible approximate the Integrals anymore by replacing them by their sum counterparts. For example, if we face only two observations for a curve, ![\hat{\theta}_r=\sum_j \phi_r(t_j)X(t_j)dt](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cc73c7c219c8a1bc4512591c6a2e2f27_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") will obviously be not an suitable estimator for ![\theta_{r}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-bfb6fdf292665f5587c5e83a6eaefd52_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") anymore.\\ Therefore we need to find new ways to apply the theory in practice. In the next part we will introduce two different approaches to solve the problem of applying an FPCA in practice while facing sparse data. The first one is a more classical approach done by Yao, Müller and Wang in \cite{Yao2005} which is closely connected to the usual FPCA method, the second one makes use of a Bayesian framework and was presented by Thompson und Rosen \cite{Thompson2008}. We will stick to the notation introduced above and assume a random design in which the sequences of timepoints ![\{t_{ij}\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-59506ffe8372f2859395542c38081c32_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") for each ![i=1,\dots,n](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-54e678223c7fb91ffd9b5e77cc1d1e6b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is sparse.

\subsection{The Yao-Müller-Wang Approach}\
As outlined before, we encounter three challenges while facing sparse data to apply a FCPA: we have to find a suitable estimator for the mean, covariancefunction and loadings. We proceed by not only assuming a continuous covariance function ![C](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f34f74d98915e33f37a086f8cbfb996a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") but rather assume that the curves ![X_1,\dots, X_n \in L^2(I)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0e605ddeba81baee2dada0a7825fe705_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") itself are smooth such that the mean-, covariance- and Eigenfunctions are smooth too. In this Setting Yao, Müller and Wang suggest the use of local linear smoothers (\cite{Fan1996}): To estimate the meanfunction ![\mu(t) := E(X(t))](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a1027b16de7a230cb1e471b46a8ebe50_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), they recommend in a first step to pool the data. By doing this one can apply a standard local polynomial estimator to smooth the pooled data points. The bandwidth for the smoothing proccedure can be chosen through standard methods, i.e. by the leave-one-out crossvalidation. Of course any other estimator ![\hat{\mu}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2631e7f6512c982b1c6dae0f4797f19c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") for the mean-function ![\mu(t) = E(X(t))](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-97814570c2ea9c20807574348a9fc3fc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") obtained by a nonparametric regression using a total of ![\sum_i T_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ce8b90367588493049b03f3d6df71af6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") pooled data points can be used as an estimator. For example we can make use of a kernel smoothing, splines or wavelets to estimate the mean function but theoretical results available assume the use of kernel- or local polynomial smoothers. To understand why the pooling works let us assume that the time points for each curve are drawn randomly on ![I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-18b5e45cb4a1ee02e81b9a980f828db8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") according to some underlying density with support on ![I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-18b5e45cb4a1ee02e81b9a980f828db8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). This assumption implies that for ![N\to \infty](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-186a1eb298695298e57ad2c65a56e5b2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the set of the total observed datapoints becomes dense over ![I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-18b5e45cb4a1ee02e81b9a980f828db8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and one is able to make use of the usual asymptotical results for nonparametric regression.\\\
Finally we need an estimator for the covariance function to get the eigenfunctions needed for the FPCA. The main idea is to imagine a surface containing sparse covariance estimates with many unknown entries. The sparse covariance estimates for the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curves in this surface are:\
\bes\
C\_i(t\_{ij},t\_{ik})=(Y\_{ij} – \hat{\mu}(t\_{ij}))(Y\_{ik} – \hat{\mu}(t\_{ik})), \qquad j,k = 1, \dots, T\_i.\
\ees\
One can easily show that given two time points ![t_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-179a35e8636cb1dc2c369e6fe83b3418_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![t_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-aeadaedc6c3ac167d2a7137ff1e8bd22_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\
\be\label{eq:2}\
E(C\_i(t\_{ij},t\_{ik})|t\_{ij},t\_{ik})=C(t\_{ij},t\_{ik}) + \sigma^2 \delta\_{jk},\
\ee\
where ![\delta_{jk}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-95ff3c43d740b9d4bdbd441752c9315b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") denotes again the Kronecker delta. So, for ![t_k=t_j](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9f1ee2da2f63f0f00c65489af43ce50a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we encounter a bias.\\\
We estimate ![C(t,s)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f47d3aa362ee2d32d59ddefc2bf0fbc9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") by applying a two dimensional smoothing procedure of the data given by ![\{C_i(t_{ij},t_{ik})\}_{i=1,\dots,N, j=1,\dots, T_i}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4a7281e0c1f549b1ef52890f76dd08db_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Because of equation \eqref{eq:2}, estimation of the covariance function may need an additional substraction of the diagonal terms to account for the bias. If we do this adjustment we obviously need an additional estimator of ![\sigma^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6a987274197f5fb6bfd3855d351bc2af_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\\
The bandwidth of the surface smoother can again be chosen by a leave-one-out crossvalidation. Note that the approach of smoothing the covariance function is different from the non sparse case with random design in which, to be able to apply the FPCA, single curves are smoothed but not the covariancefunction itself.\\\
We will denote the smoothed covariance surface by ![\hat{C}(t,s)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3a6ac861c07076997c93a8d5ba251fe1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as an estimator for ![C(s,t)= Cov(X(s),X(t))](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1bfecbe31827a0dd0766867514dd12c4_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The estimated eigenfunctions ![\hat{\gamma}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-621b1644d9381469bc760459852c2979_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and corresponding eigenvalues ![\hat{\lambda}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d5465c19f80db5bf0508a4a27863db7d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") will solve the following eigenequations:

     ![\[ \int \hat{C}(t,s) \hat{\gamma}_k(s) ds = \hat{\lambda}_k \hat{\gamma}_k(t). \]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-edb343cf2662cc80cb060c618bd3ad97_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

As mentioned before, because for each ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we only have a few observations ![Y_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-611bed68a54fa0a74f5125bc09cb8238_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), using numerical integration of ![<\hat{\phi}_k,Y_{i}>](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b16039ef04c5995a6155fa06c07b7579_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to gain estimates of the loadings ![\theta_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-557ac0658a139d91e72305a21ce11e8e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is no longer reasonable.\\\
Therefore Yao, Müller and Wang suggested another way to estimate the loadings, which they called PACE. Their proposed technique uses the assumption that the loadings ![\theta_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9cdc71499b9d1523190598bf590adeff_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are gaussian. Under this assumption, the best prediction of the FPCA loadings are given by conditional expectation given the data and the time points. Let us define

     ![\begin{align*} \tilde{Y}_i&:=(Y_{i1}, \dots , Y_{i T_i})\\ \mu_i& := (\mu(t_{i1}), \dots ,\mu(t_{i T_i}))\\ \gamma_ik &:= (\gamma_k(t_{i1}), \dots ,\gamma_k(t_{i T_i})) . \end{align*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-12904f42bdeb56661b3aa1185771740d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Then, if the loadings are gaussian, one can find that ![\tilde{\theta}_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-70071b8a25b2331c6573e09ae902a8b7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as defined below gives a best prediction of the FPCA-Loading ![\theta_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-557ac0658a139d91e72305a21ce11e8e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"):\
\bes\
\tilde{\theta}\_{ik}= E [\theta\_{ik}| \tilde{Y}\_i] = \lambda\_k \gamma^T\_i \Sigma\_{Y\_i}^{-1} (\tilde{Y}\_i -\mu\_i)\
\ees\
where ![\Sigma_{Y_i}=cov(\tilde{Y}_i,\tilde{Y}_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9d7f2f9aa472904b30fce44ce7da2ad5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), because we do not know the true values needed for calculating ![\tilde{\theta}_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-70071b8a25b2331c6573e09ae902a8b7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), we have to replace them with the corresponding estimates which we obtained in the steps before. We end up with the PACE estimates ![\hat{\theta}_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ed3a4033f4e8f3cac40ab0a3003a42_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") for ![\theta_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-557ac0658a139d91e72305a21ce11e8e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") given by:\
\bes\
\hat{\theta}\_{ik}= \hat{E} [\theta\_{ik}| \tilde{Y}\_i] = \hat{\lambda}\_k \hat{\gamma}^T\_i \hat{\Sigma}\_{Y\_i}^{-1} (\tilde{Y}\_i -\hat{\mu}\_i) ,\
\ees\
where the ![(j,k)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c61ecb31f45ff6500ffa7818634fc163_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") element of ![\hat{\Sigma}_{Y_i}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cbc7be42cfb2584fa31db4dc64376aa8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is given by ![\hat{C}(t_{ij},t_{ik})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-68494c3c3fb8d3f84e6e4c82b4a48c47_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and may be adjusted for the bias if ![t_{ij}=t_{ik}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-24a6a3b398a14c4f4ed7b4cbd0bbad31_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

\subsection{The Thompson and Rosen Approach}\
This method has a slightly different starting point compared to the method of Yao, Müller and Wang. Thompson and Rosen did not try to reconstruct the covariance matrix, but apply a Bayesian setup directly to the data with the possible aim to start a FPCA or, at least, to get smoothed curves. We consider the same model as before but following Thompson and Rosen we choose a different representation of the Functions ![Y(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-586b1a46dcba48444f3ae2af8bc31715_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") which we are going to observe in practice.\\\
For this let ![B=\{(B_1(t),\dots, B_{K}(t))\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ebac1070472812c173b5c3929ff30f4c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be the set of ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") B-spline basis functions of order ![p](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3bf85f1087e9fbed3a319341134ac1a2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") with ![M](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-10ebb71bad275c1815a8f2a8c5dea0be_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") interior knots, such that ![K=M+p](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d7fed4196d5b6bd58de9397bbeb42835_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). We suppose that ![X(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-578848d0aa3a308c973281bb55a2e9c2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![\mu(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-dfd5681b2db4588bddb2741bdcbd3922_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is smooth enough to be exactly represented with the help of those ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") basis functions. We denote by ![\beta=(\beta_1,\dots, \beta_K)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6a2a0bbb5c0c6c411db31c00e7f56ad0_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the fixed vector of the corresponding coefficients for the B-splines belonging to the decomposition of the mean function, i.e. we have:\
\bes\
\mu(t)= \sum\_{k=1}^K \beta\_k B\_k(t).\
\ees\
Additionally we assume that our unobserved curves ![X_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-82dfdeacfa845bb7956c65ca99f5765d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") can be expressed with the help of the random coefficients ![b_i=(b_{i1},\dots, b_{iK})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1779537e4e642a92c8966dd1161c8281_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in terms of the B-spline basis, i.e. we have:\
\bes\
X\_i(t)= \sum\_{r=1}^L \theta\_{ir} \gamma\_r(t) = \sum\_{k=1}^K b\_{ik} B\_k(t).\
\ees\
Under this assumption we can represent the actual curves ![\{Y_i\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-761727c6c25db96113521fc5e502a73b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as:\
\bes\
Y\_i(t) = \sum\_{k=1}^K \beta\_k B\_k(t) + \sum\_{k=1}^K b\_{ik} B\_k(t) + \epsilon\_i(t),\
\ees\
where ![\epsilon_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f0aa232b2f53bc9466aa901bd42d2ed6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is supposed to be gaussian white noise process. Note that in this representation of ![Y_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0e9a7f520f410b22833f8569092feb04_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the process ![X_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-82dfdeacfa845bb7956c65ca99f5765d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") denotes indeed a zero mean process, as the mean function has been explicitly written out.\\\
Once again, in practice, we actually observe only a few observations which we can express by\
\be\label{eq:3}\
Y\_{ij} := Y\_i(t\_j) = \sum\_{k=1}^K \beta\_k B\_k(t\_{ij}) + \sum\_{k=1}^K b\_{ik} B\_k(t\_{ij}) + \epsilon\_i(t\_j),\
\ee\
where our measurement errors ![\epsilon_i(t_j)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6c167005cba8bee024347793adc04204_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are assumed to be iid ![N(0,\sigma^2)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d95de7df39fec3b2fd2bbc4d9d4acb8e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\\
To get rid of the sums we will introduce the ![\mathbb{R}^{T_i\times K}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8afb17c488180ed66be15d94920cbf49_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") design matrix ![\mathbf{B}_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-137910fa7099d9f42270493f9a4e076e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") consisting of the B-splines basis functions in which the ![(j,k)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c61ecb31f45ff6500ffa7818634fc163_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th entry is given by ![B_k(t_{ij})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7385e1ec46f96c36b33d21fe4a754b8b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Moreover, defining ![\epsilon_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f0aa232b2f53bc9466aa901bd42d2ed6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as the vector which contains all errors ![\{\epsilon_i(t_j)\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8463f7faac8288ebfd7cc1ad428a9fa7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") for the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curve, allows us to rewrite equation \eqref{eq:3} in matrix form. We have:\
\bes\
Y\_i = \mathbf{B}\_i \beta+ \mathbf{B}\_i b\_{i}+ \epsilon\_i.\
\ees\
Expressing the individual functions in terms of a B-spline basis leads to the following expression of the actually observed covariance matrix of the function ![C(t,s)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f47d3aa362ee2d32d59ddefc2bf0fbc9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Using standard rules we find\
\bes\
Var(Y\_i)= \mathbf{B}\_i \Sigma\_b \mathbf{B}\_i^T + \sigma^2 \epsilon I,\
\ees\
where ![I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-18b5e45cb4a1ee02e81b9a980f828db8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a identity matrix of size ![T_i \times T_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-59af4dd299a8e05a3e6a5d84b537db2d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![\Sigma_b](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-134bedc31a1297fca892d1668d1f7e76_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is the covariance matrix of the random coefficients ![b](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f56d50c26583f9a035ff6b4e3c0ca5c0_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Because of the fixed ![\mathbf{B}_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-137910fa7099d9f42270493f9a4e076e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we can proceed with an usual bayesian or non-bayesian factor estimation for our mixed effect model.\\\
In practice, there is at least one problem left: the breakpoints of the B-splines are not known and have to be estimated. To solve this variable selection problem the authors suggest starting with a large pool of ![M](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-10ebb71bad275c1815a8f2a8c5dea0be_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") possible interior breakpoint candidates and assign with the help of latent indicator variables, which are contained in the vector ![\iota = (\iota_1,\dots,\iota_M)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9eebbc30d0f3c2c434c7691bde522a1c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), a positive probability that each of those breakpoints will be excluded. This leads to a more general representation of the model. Adapting the notation used in Thompson and Rosen we denote by ![\{ B_1,\dots, B_{q_\iota} \}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-65c21efe8a53920b0bcec17885ed4427_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the set of ![q_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-168ecce4d55f3720e804a9bb170c57fc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") B-spline functions determined by the breakpoints selected with the help of ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Let ![\mathbf{B}_{\iota,i}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c35b9d3f326cfa46e93669e1dd1d42d5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be the corresponding design matrix for the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th observation. By introducing these latent indicators in ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), the authors create a possibility to select the smoothing parameter automatically. They simply use the fact that smoothing with the help of B-Splines can either be done through penalization or by choosing a smaller number of basis functions. Given ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), our model can be rewritten as\
\bes\
Y\_i= \mathbf{B}\_{\iota,i} \beta\_\iota+ \mathbf{B}\_{\iota,i} b\_{\iota, i}+ \epsilon\_i\
\ees\
Now we apply a Bayesian framework, to get suitable estimators for ![\beta_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3b3ca189dd651b08fda13e9b17294ab1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![b_{\iota,i}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a7cd23854bf83a677f3523aaa4487c25_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") with the help of posterior distributions.\
\subsubsection{Prior Assumptions}\
First we will define a fine grid of ![M](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-10ebb71bad275c1815a8f2a8c5dea0be_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") interior points and two endpoints at latest at ![\min{t_{ij}}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-65bf293fcc867524c88a15a0c2dd1888_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and at least at ![\max{t_{ij}}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-5eaeb200debde4da049e5b7906012404_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The endpoints will be included in the model almost surely. This leads to ![K=M+p](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d7fed4196d5b6bd58de9397bbeb42835_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") B-spline basis functions of order ![p](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3bf85f1087e9fbed3a319341134ac1a2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). For each breakpoint there is one ![\iota_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ead2a7666c6d69d2cf4ae17bb5db0cb5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") which is used to be an indicator if the corresponding breakpoint is included, i.e. ![\iota_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ead2a7666c6d69d2cf4ae17bb5db0cb5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") equals ![1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4868771cbc422b5818f85500909ce433_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") if the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th breakpoint should be included. We will define ![q_\iota=\iota' \iota'+p](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9c07f486a4da1313862e52165c5c6c38_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as the dimension of the model by including the interior breakpoints selected via ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Conditional on ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), our model uses the set of parameter ![\zeta_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f0121030430ae75eda80c64f9a6e0a28_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") where ![\zeta_\iota= \{\beta_\iota, b_\iota, \Sigma_{b,\iota}, \sigma_\epsilon^2 \}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2ebb95ff47848c76b2607a6d92ae1439_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). With the plausible assumption that ![\beta_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3b3ca189dd651b08fda13e9b17294ab1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is the prior mean of ![b_{\gamma, i}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-adf621da8e799d35a9cdbccadfcb2fdd_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we can restate the model as\
\bes\
Y\_i=\mathbf{B}\_{\iota,i} b\_{\iota, i}+ \epsilon\_i.\
\ees\
Of course, this model is a mixed model and can be solved by a simple bayesian approach. For this, we have to assign prior distributions to our set of parameters. We assume that the following standard assumptions on the prior distributions will hold:\
\bes\
p(b\_i | \beta, \Sigma\_{b,\iota}, \iota) \sim N(\beta\_\iota, \Sigma\_{b,\iota}) \; i=1,\dots,N\
\ees\
\bes\
p(\beta\_\iota|\Sigma\_{b}, \iota) \sim N(0\_\iota, c I\_\iota)\
\ees\
where ![0_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8e7c49bdd863b193d3ec7b34d38ae270_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a vector zeros whose length is ![q_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-168ecce4d55f3720e804a9bb170c57fc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![cI](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0ad75f02c71b8cabb8202cea05319583_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the identity matrix multiplied by a predefined constant which reflects the accuracy of the prior – for example ![c](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-41a04eeea923a1a0c28094a8a4680525_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") can be chosen large to obtain a vague prior. We assume for the prior for ![\Sigma_{b}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cdbe70bf8065c3469d147d730062bf87_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), a “`default conjugate prior”‘ as proposed by Kass and Natarajan 2006, which is an inverse Wishard whose density is given by\
\bes\
p( \Sigma\_{b} | \iota) \propto | \Sigma\_{b,iota}|^{-(2q\_\iota+1)/2} exp \left\{ -\frac{q\_\iota}{2} tr(S\_\iota \Sigma\_{b,\iota}^{-1}) \right\}\
\ees\
where ![S_\iota= N \sigma_\epsilon^2 (\sum B_{\iota,i}^T B_{\iota,i} )^{-1}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-893d071264b91309e473b4d3c9816a19_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the authors suggest replacing ![\sigma_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ecdd313143ca79b543602a48e5d7dcc9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") by its estimate ![\hat{\sigma}_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a930df7122452b6fb67f13bad839fa64_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to keep the gibbs sampling simple. In fact we will use the natural estimate ![S_\iota = \frac{1}{n}b_i^Tb_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7dac1909959977d9ae02fa0d407d5812_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") of ![\Sigma_{b,\iota}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-094743ce91aab62b5c93b1a20d5487f7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in the application. Although this choice is somehow datadriven, we observed that this choice won’t influence the outcomes of the mcmc very much.\\\
The prior on the error variance ![\sigma_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ecdd313143ca79b543602a48e5d7dcc9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is an inverse gamma with parameters ![c_\epsilon](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-71e575f8ab532140c6db8c847c768da9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![d_\epsilon](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c4561196553dbbc736016bc55cdadccf_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). choosing these two parameters sufficient small will result in a vague prior.\\\
We still need to specify a prior distribution for ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Since ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a binary vector, the natural choice of a prior is the product of Bernoulli distribution with parameter ![\pi](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-26d6788550ffd50fe94542bb3e8ee615_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), which implicitly assumes that each breakpoint is included in the model independently, that is:\
\bes\
p(\iota| \pi) = \prod\_{k=1}^M \pi^{\iota\_k} (1-\pi)^{1-\iota\_k}.\
\ees\
Finally, the hyperprior on ![\pi](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-26d6788550ffd50fe94542bb3e8ee615_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a ![beta(c_\pi,d_\pi)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6068db0f1771358760d6adfc635e454_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") distribution where the parameters of the distribution can be chosen to follow a priori believes of the expected number of breakpoints to be included in model. See Kohn et al in \cite{Kohn2001} for further details.\\\
Of course, the main problem of a bayesian approach is to specify the priors. As it is often the case, our choice of the priors is not only a plausible standard approach but simplifies the corresponding mcmc sample scheme to state the posterior inferences – note for example that the prior specification in our case is stated hierarchical. For the posterior analysis, we need to draw ![(\iota, \beta, b,\sigma_\epsilon^2,\Sigma_b)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-98cd7d12933290d125cf6bc668ed9f10_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). By specifying the priors as above we can factorize\
![p(\beta,b,\Sigma_b,\iota,\pi |Y)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-91767f9a5c811b596e110f43262bda75_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") by the product of the prior distributions above and get an analytical expression for each of the factors. Furthermore we can express explicitly the conditional posterior probabilites for each parameter to draw from in the MCMC sampling scheme, hence we will make use of a gibbs sampler.\\\
\subsubsection{MCMC Sampling Scheme and Posterior Analyis}\
The MCMC sampling scheme proposed by the authors can be described as a four steps procedure:\
\begin{itemize}\
\item Sample a breakpoint index ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") where ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is uniform distributed, i.e. ![p(k)=\frac{1}{M-p}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-54313adf4d5826f57563b58b36e6602e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\item Given ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), sample ![(\iota_k,\beta,\{b_i\}|\iota_{-k},\Sigma_b, \sigma_\epsilon^2)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cbefde81655fc29e7f3becdb43dd43aa_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), where ![\iota_{-k}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8adafca969e4fa6a59374fa24492c03c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") contains of all elements but the ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th element of ![\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7ae8be8caf8a95ab84cb8b39a6f2847_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\\ This simultaneous drawing of the model parameters can be done in three successive steps using a factorization of the joint distribution:\
\begin{itemize}\
\item Sample ![\iota_k|\iota_{-k},\Sigma_b,\sigma_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-66219fb2e9674c5506fb66ba68467e52_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\item Sample ![\beta|\iota, \Sigma_b,\sigma_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9e3fb4ba6b1f08288d0deaac152fd62b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\item Sample ![\{b_i\}|\iota,\beta, \Sigma_b,\sigma_\epsilon^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-356e56c1d837ca63de552202d3098a47_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\end{itemize}\
\item Sample ![\sigma_\epsilon^2|\iota,\beta,\{b_i\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0a5d9b4d0116287c719830a0fa214816_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\item Sample ![\Sigma_b | \iota,\beta,\{b_i\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3dbaa68a02b9938fca0e2b87ab9291b6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")\
\end{itemize}\
As mentioned before, under our assumptions on the prior distributions given above, one is able to find rather complex expressions for the posterior conditional distributions to sample from in each step. We relinquish to repeat those rather annoying expressions which can be found in detail in their web appendix and proceed with the estimation of the posterior mean curves.\\\
For this suppose we have, after a proper burn in, ![D](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4b9ef1bbd23fd1b198de883813285620_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") draws of parameters. We are then able to estimate the posterior overall mean curve as:\
\bes\
\hat{\mu}(t) = \frac{1}{D} \sum\_{d=1}^D B\_{\iota,d}^T(t) \beta\_{\iota,d}^{(d)}\
\ees\
and the posterior mean of the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curve by:\
\bes\
\hat{X}\_i(t) = \frac{1}{D} \sum\_{d=1}^D B\_{\iota,d}^T(t) b\_{\iota,i}^{(d)}.\
\ees\
which we will use as our estimate for the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curve. This will result in a smooth estimate of the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curve even if we are in our sparse setting, where smoothing of individual curves using standard approaches is not feasible.\
\\Furthermore we can apply an FPCA using the posterior mean of of ![\Sigma_{b,\iota}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-094743ce91aab62b5c93b1a20d5487f7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") as an estimate for the true covariance function ![C](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f34f74d98915e33f37a086f8cbfb996a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). For this let ![\tau=\{\tau_1,\dots,\tau_p\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4fbe1934c4c1f9cc659316511ae28870_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be a dense sequence of timepoints in ![I](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-18b5e45cb4a1ee02e81b9a980f828db8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and let ![B_{\tau,\iota}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1f72874ecebc1df5bc219c2144b5febe_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be the ![p\times q_\iota](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2bbedf8a14a7f94f5d101e3f106e7fdd_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") dimensional matrix of B-Splines basis function, which are evaluated at ![\tau](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-13197f4653c1fd428a291609eb1e3b87_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). We then can estimate the covariance function ![C](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f34f74d98915e33f37a086f8cbfb996a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") by:\
\bes\
\hat{C}= \frac{1}{D} \sum\_{d=1}^D B\_{\tau,\iota}^{(d)} \Sigma\_{b,\iota} (B\_{\tau,\iota}^{(d)})^T,\
\ees\
where we once again could apply a bias-correction along the diagonal elements to account for the bias based on the measurement errors. Finally we are able to perform a standard FPCA with the help of the estimated covariance function.\
Note that the bayesian approach does not need any assumptions on sparseness at all and hence could be used for the smoothing of any curves.

\section{Simulation}\
In this section we present the results of a small monte carlo simulation to compare the two approaches when facing sparse data by measuring the expected ![L^2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2b646eceaa3ae7f10330ff53add183b2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") error of estimation and its variance, i.e. we look at\
\bes\
error = E(\int\_I (X\_i(t)-\hat{X}\_i(t))^2 dt)\
\ees\
\bes\
error\_{var} = Var(\int\_I (X\_i(t)-\hat{X}\_i(t))^2 dt).\
\ees\
We obtain ![\hat{X}_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-032450ca2e6e943631f78b1ea11662b7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in the Yao, Müller and Wang case by performing the PACE while using the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th mean posterior curve as ![\hat{X}_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-032450ca2e6e943631f78b1ea11662b7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in the Thompson and Rosen case.\\\
In each simulation we create ![N=50](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6fd4912d23b7b77a3735cafb7027927c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") curves ![X_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-998dba373bb08119e12a646c873be88c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![i=1,\dots,N](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7f0f00cebe87532bbc25efa2ec867649_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Each curve is observed over a sparse sequence of ![T_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-775d5b0840fab1ca58e1ac12c9ee64d2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") time points ![\{t_{ij}\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-59506ffe8372f2859395542c38081c32_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") which are drawn uniformly over ![I=[0,1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-62502fc62c5bb73ec8122f0e6c334cc6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The number of timepoints ![T_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-775d5b0840fab1ca58e1ac12c9ee64d2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") observed for each curve is iid. ![Po(5)+2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-38441b7f00be4ee40da9f22f0b7bb56b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), such that we expect around ![7](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-aca3387e59afe477960eabc2f23b3db5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") observational points for each curve. The actual curves ![X_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-998dba373bb08119e12a646c873be88c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are created using a cubic B-spline basis of order ![p=4](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2aa223c41817dd247f61151cfc12c810_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") over ![I=[0,1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-62502fc62c5bb73ec8122f0e6c334cc6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") with 5 equidistant interior breakpoints. In each simulation the corresponding ![9](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e99b7c7ddd421682faf67e5b5f865882_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") values of the fixed effect ![\beta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6a7605b1bcca8f1b416eaf733f34e08_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are sampled with replacement from the set ![\{-10,-9,\dots,-1,1,\dots,10\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e14364cf4edae32a41047402b7f99907_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") – note that this drawing contradicts our prior specification on ![\beta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6a7605b1bcca8f1b416eaf733f34e08_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in the section above. The random coefficients ![b_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-bafc6a170096e9ce8f24ef610ca89f3a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") belonging to the ![i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-th curve are iid. normal with mean ![\beta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6a7605b1bcca8f1b416eaf733f34e08_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and covariance matrix ![I_{9 \time 9}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f7d045fc56cd6d0972aa73b3646f988f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Moreover, the measurement error ![\epsilon_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7cba15897659074c320d441ac9aaedb7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is iid. standard normal. The observed curves ![Y_{ij}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-611bed68a54fa0a74f5125bc09cb8238_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are constructed by\
\bes\
Y\_{ij} = X\_{ij} + \epsilon\_{ij} = \sum\_{j=1}^9 B\_j(t\_{ij})b\_{ij} +\epsilon\_{ij}.\
\ees\
The results are based on only ![50](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-39e5271c62d377c7a347b4eb07f74ab2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") simulations. Moreover, for the Bayesian approach, we draw only ![1000](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1bf8f6fff8256386df3f8eca006e6c48_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") parameters and discarded the first ![500](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8ce4f218c691da59dfd93c07b15d0dcf_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") of them as the burn in.
