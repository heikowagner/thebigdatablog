In this Post we will compare different ways to model insurance data. An introduction in this field was given in a [previous post](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-admin/post.php?post=2470&action=edit). To recap, suppose we observe ![n](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b170995d512c659d8668b4e42e1fef6b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") policyholder with ![m](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6b41df788161942c6f98604d37de8098_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") covariates ![X](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d4ee28752517d6062a3ca0314890342d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). We want to model the **Loss Cost** for each policyholder. Let ![N](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-5793832f979c2268e3694c246d53b1bb_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be the number of claims over the time interval ![[0, 1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-caffaae885a1287e3dfc31bfb1cd0694_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![Y_k, \; k=1, ...,N](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f27a97bb73f80d50ba6c3d9dea0e2308_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), the amount of each claim (loss). Our aim is model the aggregated loss ![S= \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d38eb9d6ac3286cf9d5f1ec262d86a29_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Instead of Estimating ![S](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-520cb534cd5b6bed768a61515b57cb7e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") directly we use a frequency-serverity model \

     ![\[ E(S|X=x)= E(N|X=x) E(Y|X=x)\]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cf08c3be558377af222c6d871db6d7f1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

.

As benchmark to compare our method with commonly used state of the art techniques we use a GLM and a tree based method. In particular LightGBM. [LightGBM](https://neptune.ai/blog/how-to-organize-your-lightgbm-ml-model-development-process-examples-of-best-practices) is different from other gradient boosting frameworks because it uses a leaf-wise tree growth algorithm, but has a great performance used with insurance data. \

A brief introduction GAM

Recap, that in the last post we used a GLM to model frequency and severity. Here we assume that there exists some link function ![g](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d208fd391fa57c168dc0f151de829fee_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") such that for outcome ![Y](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-82606c3098bb09002088b0f6f9ffbb2a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") with independent covariates ![X](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d4ee28752517d6062a3ca0314890342d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![\mu=E(Y|X)= g^{-1}(X\beta)=g^{-1}(\theta)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4730692a2638f0b5e8ff9c473b81d4d3_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The idea of a GAM is very similar to this approach, however instead of allowing only a linear connection ![g(E(Y|X))=X\beta=\sum_i^m X_i \beta_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2a2ee5cdbfa283f2efffb9a67aac1483_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we also allow a non-linear connection ![g(E(Y|X))= \beta_0 + \sum_i^m f_i(X_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-7695c402cda63aaccc9e802ebc3b67aa_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") where ![f()](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-69ae11219de9f4e5d247f9f5622647b5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is an unknown function. A way to model ![f()](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-69ae11219de9f4e5d247f9f5622647b5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is using splines. A natural cubic spline with knots ![\zeta_1<\dots<\zeta_K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-29104320cbfbad18c2bfaaaf145d8820_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a piecewise polynomial of order 4, with additional constrains such that the the function is linear beyond the boundary knots, represented with ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") Basis functions ![N_1(t),\dots, N_K(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-dffbdae0b0ee3ef52458b7e212a01d23_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") such that ![N_1(t)= 1,\; N_1(t)= t,\; N_{k+2}(t)= d_k(t)-d_{K-1}(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-23a5dd3627f78cb2218ab077a7d978d5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), \
![d_k(t) = \frac{(t-\zeta_k)^3_+ - (t-\zeta_K)^3_+}{\zeta_K-\zeta_k}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-bcd63aa19983841d7f615ae6d7a5ee53_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The natural cubic spines with fitting coefficients ![\theta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-356a08e839ab6974a16448e16e56745d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is given by ![f(X)= \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^K N_j(X) \theta_j](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3fb0017184b19364baf6ea61e0cfb5eb_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") another often used spline representation is the [B-Spline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-spline) which will result in different Basis functions. Plugging in our initial GAM representation, leads to \

     ![\[g(E(Y|X))= \beta_0 + \sum_i^m f_i(X_i) =  \beta_0 + \sum_i^m  \sum_j^K  N_j(X_i) \theta_{ij}\]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c941105e6fe6fd6e264feb950feb2a6d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

\
Which can be considered as an usual GLM, but with ![m K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-92d2069c5308b0b713f8c7a5396dd531_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") coefficients instead of ![m](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6b41df788161942c6f98604d37de8098_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). GAM with Jumps and Lasso

In this section we will additionally allow ![f()](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-69ae11219de9f4e5d247f9f5622647b5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to jump at each knot by ![f(X)= \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^K N_j(X) \theta_j  + \sum_{k=K+1}^{2K} \mathbf{I}(X<\zeta_k) \theta_k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e9b4aea5e3739dbadb268fd981d7d8dc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Since we want to avoid having artificial discontinuities at every knot as well to control the smoothness of ![f()](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-69ae11219de9f4e5d247f9f5622647b5_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), we use the elastic net is a regularization. Elastic net combines the ![L_1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6affe041739f78a044af6d3f56d6b659_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![L_2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-84db227018e3228ebc26b639aa94bbd0_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") penalties of the lasso and ridge methods. While the ![L_1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6affe041739f78a044af6d3f56d6b659_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") penalty term will cause unnecessary regression coefficients to be exactly zero which as an effect will keep the number of jumps to a minimum, the ![L_2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-84db227018e3228ebc26b639aa94bbd0_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") penalty term will control the smoothness of the spline. This leads to a loss function of the form\

     ![\[min_{\theta} \; l(Y|f(X), \theta) + \lambda_1 ||\theta||^2 + \lambda_2  ||\theta||_1.\]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0788417b6e46d63629e20bbfafe6c151_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

\
Here ![l(Y|X, \theta)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e7e46eb58938480ab6e8cb6bbd9992aa_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is the GLM loss as to be found [here](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/frequency-severity-modeling-in-consideration-of-covid-19-induced-effects/).
