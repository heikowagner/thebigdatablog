To explain the registration problem i will start with an example. In Figure 1 the [pinch force dataset](https://rdrr.io/cran/fda/man/pinch.html) is shown, to collect the data a group of 20 subjects were asked to press a button as hard if they can after they hear a sound signal. The pressure was then recorded every 2 milliseconds, resulting in 151 observations. Since reaction times of the subjects differ we can clearly see some shift in the curves reflecting the pressure. The problem with this kind of shifted data is now that even very simple statistical measures, like mean or variance, are not meaningful. To see this take a look at the blue curve which is the sample mean ![\hat{x}(t)=\sum_{i=1}^N x_i(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a527e92c2b8ed1e034f061ea50989e7c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). It is visible, that the mean curve does not reflect the shape of the sample curves and even worse the highest point of this curve is smaller than the smallest sample curve. To obtain information about the mean pressure this curve is therefore a bad measure. In case of the pinch fore data an obvious way to fix this problem is to align the curves at certain landmarks, for example the peaks of each curve. This is landmark registration is for example covered by Bookstein (1978, 1997), Kneip and Gasser(1992) or Gasser and Kneip (1995). However, Landmark registration has certain drawbacks. Considering more complex problems defining the Landmarks becomes ugly very fast, especially when working with more than one spatial dimension. It is also not clear how to choose the Landmarks, consider for example curves where some are wider than others, in this case sometimes also inflection points are used as landmarks. This then leads to a methods which rely on minimizing a distance ![d(x_i \circ h_i ,\gamma)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3f68427a260a7eb21f66460a51113ab7_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") between registered functions ![y_i (t) = x_i (h_i (t))](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1563bd8e4a3973fa392ef1d544db7e67_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and a template ![\gamma(t)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f1694b647ab256b7735228b2879d9a58_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), for example one curve out of the sample. See Sakoe and Chiba (1978). Ramsay (1998), Ramsay and Li (1998), or Kneip et al. (2000) for more insights. This strategy works very well in many situations but also has severe problems. Consider for example a sample of curves where some curve have one peak while others have two. A registration method that minimizing a distance between the curves and a one peaked template, will then then to pinch the curves with two peaks, see Ramsay and Li (1998).

An alternative approach was developed by Kneip and Ramsay (2008) and Wagner and Kneip (2019). Where registration was considered as a tool for statistical analysis. Whenever the random functions ![x_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c8700e0258243116de0d4f288e2e3b44_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") possess “bounded shape variation”, then there exists a finite ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and warping functions ![h_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c59cfc50326c96eff701e9f2dda48f94_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") such that with probability 1

     ![\[x_i(h_i(t)) = \sum_{j=1}^K a_{ij} \gamma_j(t)\]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d92c2bd1e9a1bba478effe5ed2835ed8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

for some basis functions ![\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e9f3425a02169d7ab20d1ab2d696e748_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and individually different coefficients ![a_{i1},\dots,a_{iK}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-176431cf64850a38d0d764655fb7111c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

## Registration to a template Space

In the following we will consider not some basis functions ![\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e9f3425a02169d7ab20d1ab2d696e748_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") but will assume a carefully pre-chosen basis system. In particular for a smooth, at least twice continuously differentiable function ![x](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ede05c264bba0eda080918aaa09c4658_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"):

- ![\tau^x= \{ t | x'(t)=0, x''(t)\neq 0 \}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b3ed63f508bfff0c09836d8c3c933b02_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")
- ![q(x)=|\tau^x|](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a340b324931ed41e1443433c9273bae0_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (cardinality)
- ![Q(x)=(x(0),x(\tau^x_1),\dots,x(\tau^x_{q(x)}),x(1))^T\in\mathbb{R}^{q(x)+2}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c6de862fe5403279731eda2dca6d740a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

According to Wagner and Kneip (2019), for some integer ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") let ![L_K=span\{\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_K\}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ad94ca02e16e1cd2348e8b6ee9e7ba7f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") denote a ![K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ea9c87a513e4a72624155d392fae86e2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-dimensional linear function space generated by smooth, twice continuously differentiable basis functions ![\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_j](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e3fb49f010197662b68f8b87b207a2ea_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") on ![[0,1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-25b6d943ab489c05a3dbd5ea29087a48_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). If
![\mathbb{P}(Q(x_i)\in \{Q(\gamma)|\gamma\in L_K\})=1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9cbbbcaa062cfd66eaf2e797992b89b9_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") there then exists a registration procedure such that
![\mathbb{P}(x_i\circ h_i\in L_K)=1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-06ff95401b68c670e015f6ddb8bc9dbf_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

The important finding here is that, for smooth functions, ![Q(x_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-809cda99254fc972161c8d24e928a2da_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is essentially the only structural feature of ![x_i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c8700e0258243116de0d4f288e2e3b44_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") which is invariant against strictly monotone transformations. It is thus the driving force of identifiability and holds all information needed when registering to a pre-specified space ![L_K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f376eb1624f822a0b4b4761793cc60f3_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). More precisely, for given ![L_K](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f376eb1624f822a0b4b4761793cc60f3_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") we will consider the following minimization problem

(1)    ![\begin{eqnarray*} S_i(L_K)= \min_{\alpha \in \mathbb{R}^K} || Q(x_i)-Q( \sum_j^K \gamma_j \alpha_j ) ||^2. \end{eqnarray*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-87ab5c0393a4eb6024b0441bc47cb328_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Let ![\alpha^*](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cbcc67088f2cef10e188b5f04bee3721_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be a minimizer of ([1](#id3586267572)) and ![y_i=\sum_j^K \gamma_j \alpha^*_j](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-27c568795f3cff4f2243cfda7dfd4afa_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Under the assumption that ![\mathbb{P}(Q(x_i)\in \{Q(\gamma)|\gamma\in \Lcal_K\})=1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-00275fcb79a7d15f3225e5022ea04381_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![Q(x_i)=Q(y_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f03dbc7f66022edc6f5f0eaec7731785_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![S_i(L_K) = 0](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a0a85ac35d567f9bd1d558a93c49dcd8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"),([1](#id3586267572)) always has a solution. This assumption is indeed not very restricting, a ![K\leq q(x_i)+2](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a8313372e374bf029bbf5d6d47c12d82_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") dimensional function space fulfilling this requirement can always be constructed an example is a suitable basis of all polynomials of order ![q(x_i)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-758b561dada7e449688039be4f96694f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") on ![[0,1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-25b6d943ab489c05a3dbd5ea29087a48_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

## Derive the Warpingfunctions
