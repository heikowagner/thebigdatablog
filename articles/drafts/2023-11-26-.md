Functional regression is a powerful statistical technique that extends the principles of classical regression analysis to the realm of functional data. Unlike traditional regression, which deals with scalar or vector-valued predictors and responses, functional regression operates on data that is naturally represented as functions or curves. This form of data is prevalent in various domains, including biology, finance, signal processing, and many others, where observations are collected over continuous domains, such as time. Let the covariate ![X= (X(t), t \in [0,1])](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-75e2983a5e08dba202a6c03e9723948e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be an at least twice continuously differentiable random function defined wlog. on an interval ![[0,1]](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-25b6d943ab489c05a3dbd5ea29087a48_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![Y \in \mathbb{R}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e0359cd9111b54530a1a8797a0bde70b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the corresponding the response. For simplicity we assume centred random variables, i.e. ![E(X)=0, E(Y)=0](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-07dcbb93fe4ffdcb4ef653ccf60d2269_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") beside we require bounded second moments ![E(Y^2)< \infty](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-75bcf6402bf367d78646362c5be79389_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![\int_0^1 E(X(t)^2)< \infty](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b833cf07a5034c64b20d488110861c59_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). (1)    ![\begin{align*}Y= \int_0^1 \beta(t) X( t ) dt.\end{align*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-09551722d4cc611675e65906f7dea623_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

(3)    ![\begin{align*}(\Gamma \beta)(t) = g(t).\end{align*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6c301cd3a809e4724c072bed910f853_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

([3](#id879327147)) has a solution if and only if ![\Gamma](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4f420945e64069f30b66c3d17e2f98ac_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is invertible, for ![\lambda_i>0 \; \forall i](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-31f9fa92f49ce0a0e94f7ec089ef0309_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the unique solution is then given by\

Popular ways to tackle this problem, is to trade the uniqueness for the stability of the a solution.\

An argument to support this approach is often based on the “best basis” property by selecting the eigenfunctions as a basis such that the most variance of the data is explained.\
However there is no garantee, that the “best basis” to represent ![X](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d4ee28752517d6062a3ca0314890342d_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is also the best basis to describe ![\beta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6a7605b1bcca8f1b416eaf733f34e08_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

## 1. Defining Functional Attention

Instead of using a basis which is best to explain the variability of X, we are going to use a transformer to derive the “best basis” to model ![\beta](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-b6a7605b1bcca8f1b416eaf733f34e08_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").\
Most applications that use transformer, like supposed in the “Attention is all you Need”, are dealing with some kind of language tasks.\
However, there is a growing literature that use transformer for timeseries analysis \cite{wen2023transformers}, \cite{LIM20211748}.\
Attention is a central element in the transformer architecture. In traditional sequence-to-sequence models, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, capturing dependencies across elements of a sequence often requires sequential processing. This sequential nature can lead to slow training and inference times. Transformers, on the other hand, leverage the attention mechanism to parallelize the processing of elements in a sequence, making them highly efficient and effective in capturing long-range dependencies.

One possible solution to give the model some sense of order is to add a piece of information to each word about its position in the sentence. We call this “piece of information”, the positional encoding.

The first idea that might come to mind is to assign a number to each time-step within the [0, 1] range in which 0 means the first word and 1 is the last time-step. Could you figure out what kind of issues it would cause? One of the problems it will introduce is that you can’t figure out how many words are present within a specific range. In other words, time-step delta doesn’t have consistent meaning across different sentences.

Another idea is to assign a number to each time-step linearly. That is, the first word is given “1”, the second word is given “2”, and so on. The problem with this approach is that not only the values could get quite large, but also our model can face sentences longer than the ones in training. In addition, our model may not see any sample with one specific length which would hurt generalization of our model.

We need no positional encoding and can take [0,1] 1 d vector!
