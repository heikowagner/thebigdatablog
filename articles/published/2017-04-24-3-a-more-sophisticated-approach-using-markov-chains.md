## 1. Generalize the Procedure

The [very simple Educated Guess Procedure](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/1-thougts-about-passwords/) is not only very simple, the procedure is also very unrealistic. At most the assumption that ![x_i, \, i=1,\dots,E](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-652d3c00f2b8026af6943d0bc20f8752_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") are independent and identically distributed random variables is not satisfied in practice. Consider for example ![x^*_1=l, x^*_2=o, x^*_3=v](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f0872415f04eb37012657985992d630b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") where observed, the probability that ![x^*_4=e](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-529fc954b29ae445e5974c47822cad0f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") should then be higher then ![x_4=a](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-caa412f9bce429a58eb55e51147ed85a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), even though [our analyze](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/coding-the-educated-guess-procedure/) reveals that assuming independence ![P(x_i= a|\mathbb{L})> P(x_i= e|\mathbb{L})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9cc89872d25f96137a45113739d590c1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). Another recommendation in terms of “how to create a good password” given by experts is often to use the first letter of some Phrase. For example “**I** **l**ike **t**o **e**at **10** **r**ed **a**pples **i**n **t**he **m**orning” will become “Ilte10raitm”. Look’s pretty random at first glance, however language is not random and thus such passwords are weaker (and easier to detect by the following method) than truly random passwords. To model these issues we introduce\

(1)    ![\begin{equation*} P(x_i= x^*_i|x_{i-1}= x^*_{i-1}, \dots,x_{i-k}= x^*_{i-k} ) = P(x_i= x^*_i|x_{i-1}= x^*_{i-1}, \dots,x_1= x^*_1 ) \end{equation*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4c4912e6ac2f55d9f7a2943591ebc9bd_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

where the probability to observe a certain letter depends on the previous ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") letters. One usually speaks in this context of a [Markov chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markov_chain) of order ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), further it an be seen that our previous attempt is a special case of ([1](#id3387596268)) where ![k=0](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d9e234749ecc0a2852f141f388e2d253_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). In linguistics, such models are known as ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-grams.

## 2. Generate better “guessed” Passwords

To get estimates we will thus relay on ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-gram counting, to do so in advance we need some additional notation. Let ![\pi_k: \mathbb{A} \rightarrow \mathbb{A}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-038941ddbf0783b96b6b049c8f23e26c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") be a permutation with repetitions of length ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and with ![\sum_{\pi_k(\mathbb{A}) }](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-464462003dcf17eb40f9c9a1707c614c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the sum over all possible permutations is meant. An estimator for the probability of a specific permutation ![\pi^*_{k,i}:= (x^*_i, \dots, x^*_{i-k})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f0cfc9f66c8811933940d17aaf4f8422_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![\pi^*_{k,i,-1}:= (x^*_{i-1}, \dots, x^*_{i-k})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-031d89ec363280d0bce7933f5601e182_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") can then be derived by\

(2)    ![\begin{equation*} \hat{P}(x_i= x^*_i|x_{i-1}= x^*_{i-1}, \dots,x_{i-k}= x^*_{i-k} )  = \frac{ \sum_{i=k+1}^\infty \# (X \in \mathbb{L} |x_{i}\dots x_{i-k}=x^*_{i}\dots x^*_{i-k})}{ \sum_{i=k+1}^\infty \# (X \in \mathbb{L} |x_{i-1}\dots x_{i-k}=x^*_{i-1}\dots x^*_{i-k})} \end{equation*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f16d691bd7e08417e6a6499c8f723946_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

however, additional smoothing is needed especially if we assume an alphabet with many special characters because there is a chance that some ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-grams are not observed in our training sample. Such ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-grams will then be assigned with a probability 0 and will never be considered in any generation procedure, however in reality these ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-grams are for sure possible. We use Good-Turing smoothing, this means for a specific permutation ![\pi^*_k= (x^*_1, \dots, x^*_k)](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-f3878763843b8f03beafe729b43bbedc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), each count ![r(\pi^*_k) =\#(X \in \mathbb{L} |x_{1}\dots x_{k-1}=x^*_{1}\dots x^*_{k-1})](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-6d338a4a265d0b4028d9cdd2faf51913_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![n_{r(\pi^*_k)}= \sum_{\pi_k(\mathbb{A}) } \mathbf{1}(r(\pi^*_k)=  r(\cdot) )](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-19a7e5d7c89cc83c55070cfc84177258_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") the number of k-grams observed ![r](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c409433a9e2dfcdb83360a974d243f18_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") times, an adjusted count ![r^*(\pi^*_k) =(r(\pi^*_k) +1)\frac{n_r(\pi^*_k) +1}{n_r(\pi^*_k) }](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-07701f8496277b01bcea430c6181e775_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is computed. A Good-Turing smoothed version of [2](#id386190640) is then given by\

(3)    ![\begin{equation*} \hat{P}_T(x_i= x^*_i|x_{i-1}= x^*_{i-1}, \dots,x_{i-k}=\frac{\sum_{i=k+1}^\infty r^*(\pi^*_{k,i})}{\sum_{i=k+1}^\infty r^*(\pi^*_{k,i,-1})} \end{equation*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e93b84bd09f3963f60b653e7b02ef52e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Still we are facing possibly unrealistic assumptions, for example that the length of the password does not depend on the used letters. Another hard assumption is that we are always facing a ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-gram. A further generalization taking this issue into account would be to use a Variable-order Markov model with allows for different ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")‘s depending on a given observation.

To start the Markov Chain we need an initial state, therefore randomly draw ![k-1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1a4c925a5ad5141d0726a09015ceadcd_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") letters according with the following probabilities

(4)    ![\begin{equation*} \hat{P}_T(x_{1}= x^*_{1},x_{2}= x^*_{2} \dots, x_{k-1}= x^*_{k-1} )  = \frac{ r^*(x^*_{1}, \dots, x^*_{k-1} )}{ \# (X \in \mathbb{L} )}. \end{equation*}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-121f3d69afee352099979c713f89b96f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

If we assume that ![|\mathbb{A}|= n](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a884008531288f4dd11d595c7f74bf27_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"),  then this will give ![(k-1)^n](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2aaf23c92779bbbf943ffc22322b0704_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") possible intal states according to a certain alphabet ![\mathbb{A}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4fdb36901bfeee1bb4b059305c252e86_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). We also need to construct a list of containing all possible transition probability given by ([3](#id3842590838)) between the ![k](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")-grams. Combinatorics then tells us that there exist ![k^n](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-52d4ae10414ed3d6c415a4bcc681067c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") permutations. Using these lists we can then construct a Markov chain using the estimated probabilities. The Markov chain is then interrupted after ![E^*-k-1](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ff7b991a976f5e2c6dc13e8bc0b50207_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") steps where ![E^*](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1c253dc9ae4dfe71d14c7471e4aae861_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") ist drawn, like in the [previous approach](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/1-thougts-about-passwords/), from ![\hat{f}_E(E^*)= \frac{\# (X \in \mathbb{L} |E=E^*)}{\# \mathbb{L}}](https://www.thebigdatablog.com/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-350afe54fad6baf73244ef4548f2e64e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").
